01

Abstract

Cancer diagnosis is a setting where misclassification errors are deeply asymmetric: a false negative can delay treatment, while a false positive can trigger unnecessary biopsies and patient distress. Standard neural network classifiers achieve strong accuracy on histopathology benchmarks but produce overconfident softmax probabilities that do not reflect genuine uncertainty.

Gaussian Process Classification offers a principled posterior over latent functions, but MCMC inference at PCam scale (N ≈ 2.6 × 10⁵) has historically been intractable. We make exact MCMC-based GPC practical by combining three pieces: accelerated Randomly Pivoted Cholesky to compress the kernel, Hamiltonian Monte Carlo in non-centered low-rank coordinates, and a pivots-as-inducing-points predictive rule that turns the test cost into O(k²) per point. On PCam and CAMELYON17, the resulting classifier improves calibration over both SVGP and the strongest neural baseline, while reducing missed cancer cases.

The contribution is not just a faster sampler — it is showing that calibrated, full-data Bayesian classification is now a deployable option in domains where uncertainty is part of the diagnosis.

03

Background & motivation

Effective deployment of any classifier in a diagnostic setting depends not only on raw accuracy, but on calibrated probabilistic predictions that pathologists can use to triage uncertain cases for human review. Standard neural network classifiers, despite high accuracy on histopathology benchmarks, are known to produce overconfident softmax outputs that do not faithfully reflect uncertainty — particularly on out-of-distribution patches.

Gaussian Process Classification: limitations

GPC is a natural alternative: it provides a principled posterior over latent functions and yields calibrated predictive distributions that can flag genuinely ambiguous cases. The challenge is computational. Solving GPC requires inference over the posterior of the latent function, and two families of methods are commonly used:

  • Stochastic Variational Gaussian Processes (SVGP) approximate the posterior with a parametric Gaussian via variational inference. Fast and scalable, but the mean-field assumption systematically underestimates posterior variance, hurting calibration.
  • Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) samples the true posterior asymptotically, capturing full posterior uncertainty and giving the best-calibrated predictions — but it has historically been computationally infeasible at the scale required for medical imaging benchmarks.

Recent advances in randomized numerical linear algebra change this picture. The dense kernel matrix at N = 262,144 would require approximately 550 GB at float64, and exact Cholesky is O(N³) — both clearly intractable. Low-rank methods make MCMC GPC reachable at this scale for the first time.

For both datasets, image patches are converted into embeddings using Phikon, a frozen pathology-specific transformer encoder. Phikon embeddings are then projected to d = 512 dimensions using PCA fit on the training set only. The GP and the neural baselines all operate on these shared embeddings, so the comparison isolates the classifier and uncertainty model rather than the image encoder.

05

Method

Our pipeline replaces the dense kernel operations that block MCMC GPC at scale with a low-rank reparameterization that preserves posterior fidelity. There are three pieces.

iKernel

Accelerated RPCholesky

A recent advancement in randomized numerical linear algebra. It builds a low-rank factor F ∈ ℝN×k such that FF⊤ ≈ K, by greedily selecting k pivot columns of K via randomized importance sampling. The full N × N matrix is never materialized; only k columns of K are ever queried, reducing the kernel-evaluation cost from O(N²) to O(Nk).

FF⊤ = K:,P · K−1PP · K⊤:,P, with pivot set P selected greedily by RPCholesky. Nyström-type approx.
iiSampler

HMC in non-centered coordinates

We reparameterize the GP latent function as f = Fν with ν ∼ 𝓝(0, Ik), and run Hamiltonian Monte Carlo over ν. Sampling in this k-dimensional space (k = 200) instead of the N-dimensional space (N = 262,144) makes the posterior well-conditioned and HMC tractable. Gradients of the log-posterior are O(Nk) per leapfrog step, dominated by a single matrix–vector product F⊤(y − σ(Fν)).

f = Fν,      ν ∼ 𝓝(0, Ik) Non-centered reparameterization
iiiPrediction

Pivots-as-inducing-points

The RPCholesky pivots P are reused at test time as inducing points: the predictive mean for a test point x* is f*mean ≈ k⊤*,P · K−1PP · fP. This avoids the O(ntest · N) kernel evaluation that would dominate predictive cost in a naive Woodbury-based approach, replacing it with O(k²) per test point — a ~1500× reduction at PCam scale.

Experimental progression

We organize the experiments as a progression from controlled, small-set validation to full-scale histopathology evaluation. Across all of them we track Expected Calibration Error (ECE), Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL), AUROC, AUPRC, Brier score, sensitivity and specificity, accuracy, and full confusion matrices.

Experiment 1 · Verifying ARPCholesky speed-up on a PCAM subset

We generated a synthetic binary classification dataset with N = 2,000 points using two Gaussian-like blobs centered near the y-axis (left blob labeled 0, right labeled 1) and compared the standard GP-MCMC dense-kernel baseline against accelerated RPCholesky approximations at ranks k ∈ {20, 50, 100}.

Dense kernel baseline N/A 0.0230 0.0230
Accelerated RPCholesky 100 0.0098 2.3× 0.0700 5.0×
Accelerated RPCholesky 50 0.0115 2.0× 0.0620 5.7×
Accelerated RPCholesky 20 0.0037 6.2× 0.0680 5.2×

Even at this small scale the speed-up is consistent: factorization time drops by up to 6.2×, and total MCMC wall-clock by up to 5.7×. The speed-up grows with N, which is what makes the full-PCam runs in later experiments feasible.

Experiment 2 · Identifying the best NN-based baseline

To make sure the comparison wasn't rigged against neural networks, we benchmarked three NN architectures on PCam to find the strongest neural baseline:

  1. ViT-S, a Vision Transformer (Small) trained from scratch.
  2. Phikon embeddings → MLP, the same embeddings used by our GP, fed into a Residual MLP head.
  3. U-Net trained on flattened image patches.

Across ECE, compute time, NLL, false negatives, and accuracy, the U-Net trained on flattened images yielded the best overall performance and was carried forward as the NN baseline for the final comparison.

Experiment 4 · SVGP vs best NN vs our method

Final comparison of the GPyTorch SVGP baseline, the U-Net NN baseline (winner of Experiment 2), and our low-rank HMC GP — on both PCam and CAMELYON17.

Calibration
Lower ECE on both datasets.

The HMC posterior's confidence scores match empirical correctness more closely than SVGP's mean-field approximation or the U-Net's softmax outputs.

Sharpness
Lower NLL across the board.

Our model is less confidently wrong — it concentrates probability mass on correct labels more reliably than either baseline.

Clinical safety
Fewer false negatives.

The low-rank HMC GP misses fewer cancer-positive cases at the default operating threshold — the failure mode that matters most in screening.

Discrimination
Highest accuracy.

Calibration gains do not come at the cost of classification performance. The trade-off is wall-clock training time, which is slower than SVGP.

07

Conclusion

Solving the bottleneck

MCMC-based full Gaussian Process Classification is now tractable on histopathology-scale datasets, removing what has historically been the dominant computational barrier to deploying calibrated Bayesian classifiers at scale. The kernel matrix is never materialized, the sampler operates in k dimensions instead of N, and prediction is O(k²) per point.

An alternative when Bayesian uncertainty matters

MCMC GPC isn't as fast as SVGP or NN baselines — but for applications where calibrated uncertainty has real value, such as medical diagnosis and decision-making under risk, the calibration advantages we demonstrate over SVGP and NN baselines make the method worth using in domains where Bayesian uncertainty matters.

Future work

  • Extend the method to multi-class medical classification (e.g. tumor subtyping).
  • Stronger hyper-parameter search and architecture tuning for the NN baselines.
  • Reconcile the HMC sigmoid likelihood with the SVGP probit likelihood for cleaner head-to-head comparisons.
  • Investigate whether the low-rank approximation acts as implicit regularization beyond PCam / CAMELYON17.
  • Hybrid architectures combining learned image features with principled Bayesian probabilistic heads.
